# Stage map — match your round to the right investor

Targeting fails most often on **stage mismatch**: pitching a growth fund at
pre-seed, or a tiny angel for a $5M Series A. Use this to filter. Numbers are
rough US/global-startup norms as of the mid-2020s — **verify current bands per
fund**, they vary by market and cycle.

| Stage | Typical raise | Typical check (per investor) | Who writes it | What they need to see |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-seed** | $100K–$1M | $10K–$250K | Angels, operator angels, pre-seed micro-funds, accelerators, syndicates | Founder + insight + early signal (prototype, waitlist, design partner, LOIs) |
| **Seed** | $1M–$4M | $100K–$1M (lead $500K–$2M) | Seed funds, multi-stage seed arms, super-angels | Working product + early traction or strong wedge; a believable path to Series A metrics |
| **Series A** | $5M–$15M+ | $2M–$10M (lead takes most) | Series A funds, multi-stage funds | Real, repeatable traction: revenue/usage growth, retention, early GTM that scales |

## How stage changes your target list

- **Pre-seed**: weight toward **angels and accelerators**. They write fast, care
  about *you* and the insight, and tolerate raw metrics. A single believer can
  anchor the round. Cast wider; warm intros matter but cold-but-relevant works.
- **Seed**: you need a **lead**. Identify funds that *lead* at seed (not just
  follow) in your sector. Separate "lead" targets from "fill the round" angels.
- **Series A**: targeting narrows to funds whose A check and ownership model fit
  your raise size. Warm intros and a metrics-driven deck matter most; cold
  outreach converts worse here, so prioritize the `warm-intro` path.

## Check-size sanity test

Your round size ÷ a realistic lead check ≈ how many lead-capable firms you
actually need. If you're raising $3M seed and a target writes $50K, they're a
*fill*, not a lead — tier accordingly. Don't put fills where you need a lead.

## Other axes that gate fit

- **Ownership targets**: many funds need 10–20% ownership; that dictates the
  check they *must* write and whether your round can accommodate them.
- **Geography / jurisdiction**: some funds only invest in their region or can't
  take certain entity structures. Confirm before spending a warm intro.
- **Sector mandate**: a generalist fund with two portfolio cos in your space is
  often better than a "fintech fund" that hasn't deployed in a year. Recency of
  relevant deals > the label on the fund.
