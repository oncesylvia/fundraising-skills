# Narrative frameworks

Tools for building each beat. Use them to sharpen — not to fill in a Mad Libs
template. The best pitch sounds like a founder talking, not a framework.

## The one-liner formulas

Try a few, pick what's clearest:

- **Job-to-be-done:** "We help <customer> <do the job> without <pain>."
- **Category + twist:** "<Familiar category>, but <the key difference>."
- **The X for Y:** "The <known thing> for <new domain>." (Powerful for warm
  intros because it's instantly forwardable — but only if the analogy is honest.)
- **Outcome-first:** "We get <customer> from <bad state> to <good state>."

Rules: a smart outsider must understand it; no buzzwords; under ~15 words.

## Why-now (the most-skipped, high-signal beat)

Investors fund *timing*. Name the specific shift that opened the window:
- A **technology** unlock (cost curve, new capability, new infra).
- A **behavior** shift (how customers now work/buy).
- A **regulatory** change.
- A **market** dislocation (an incumbent stumble, a budget moving).

Test: "Why couldn't this have been built 3 years ago, and why will it be too
late in 3 more?" If there's no good answer, the urgency is missing — say so.

## The wedge vs. the vision

- The **wedge** is the sharp, narrow entry: one acute pain, one beachhead
  customer, one feature that's a painkiller (not a vitamin).
- The **vision** is where the wedge expands (land-and-expand).
- Lead with the wedge for credibility, gesture at the vision for upside. Founders
  who lead with a grand platform and no wedge read as unfocused.

## Traction framing

- **Rate of change > absolute level.** "30% MoM for 4 months" beats a bare "$X."
- **Pick the one metric that matters** for your model (ARR/growth, retention,
  engagement, LOIs) and lead with it.
- **Pre-revenue?** Use the strongest *leading* indicator honestly: a signed
  design partner, a waitlist with intent, a sharp insight from lived experience.
  Being early is fundable; being inflated is not.

## Why-you (founder–market fit)

The question behind every other question. Strong answers come from:
- **Lived the problem** — you were the customer / built this internally before.
- **Unfair insight** — you know something non-obvious and true about the space.
- **Unfair advantage** — distribution, a key relationship, deep domain/tech edge.
- **Track record** — you've executed in this domain before.
One specific, true sentence beats a paragraph of credentials.

## The raise beat

- **Amount + use of funds tied to milestones:** "Raising $X to get from <today's
  metric> to <the metrics that unlock the next round> over <N months>."
- Show you know what the *next* stage requires (see `investor-targeting`'s
  stage-map) and that this round credibly gets you there.

## Forwardability test (the quality bar)

After one hearing, can an investor accurately retell your story to their
partners? If not, it's too complex or too vague. Tighten until it's repeatable —
your pitch has to survive being relayed by someone who isn't you.

## Tailoring by audience (same spine, shifted emphasis)

- **Thesis-driven VC:** lead with why-now + wedge + market shape.
- **Operator angel:** lead with why-you + the insight; they back people fast.
- **Strategic investor:** lead with the wedge's overlap with their world + pull.
- **Accelerator:** lead with team + the speed of learning, not polish.

Shift emphasis, never the facts.
