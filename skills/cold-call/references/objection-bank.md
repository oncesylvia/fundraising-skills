# Objection bank — investor calls

Pattern for every objection: **acknowledge → answer briefly and honestly →
bridge back** to traction or the next step. Never defensive. "Good question" +
a crisp answer signals a founder who's thought about it. Adapt to the founder's
real situation — don't read these verbatim, and never answer with a claim that
isn't true.

## "It's too early for us."
> Totally fair — what would you need to see to get interested? <Then either:
> "We're closer than it looks: <metric>", or "Makes sense — can I keep you
> posted and come back when we hit <their bar>?"> A "not yet" you nurture beats
> a "no." Get the specific milestone and log it in the pipeline.

## "What's your traction / the numbers aren't there yet."
> Here's the honest picture: <real numbers>. What I'd point to is <the trend or
> the leading indicator that matters>: <e.g. 30% MoM for 4 months / 94%
> retention / 9 design partners>. Lead with the rate of change, not the absolute.

## "The market's too small / TAM question."
> The wedge is <narrow entry>, but the expansion is <adjacent surface>: once
> we're the system of record for <X>, we grow into <Y, Z>. Show the land-and-
> expand path, not a top-down "1% of a huge number."

## "What about <big incumbent> / why won't they just do this?"
> They're structured around <legacy model / different buyer / margin
> conflict>, which is exactly why they haven't. Our wedge is <thing they won't
> prioritize>. We win <segment> first while that's true.

## "How is this defensible / what's the moat?"
> Early on it's execution speed and <specific advantage: data, distribution,
> workflow lock-in>. Over time the moat is <accumulating asset>. Be honest that
> seed-stage moats are nascent — investors know this; conviction about the path
> matters more than claiming an impregnable moat today.

## "Who else is in the round? / Do you have a lead?"
> <If yes:> <Name/type> is <committed/soft-circled> for <amount>. <If no, don't
> fake it:> We're early in the process and being deliberate about the lead — I
> wanted to talk to you because <specific fit>. Honesty here; investors check.
> Never invent commitments — it's the fastest way to lose a deal.

## "Why you / why this team?"
> <Founder–market fit in one or two true lines: the lived problem, the unfair
> insight, relevant track record.> This is the question behind every other one —
> have a sharp, specific answer.

## "Send me the deck and I'll take a look." (the polite brush-off)
> Happy to — I'll send it today. To make it useful, could we put 20 minutes on
> the calendar next week to walk through it? Convert a vague "send it" into a
> concrete next step, or it dies in the inbox.

## "What's the valuation / how much are you raising?"
> We're raising <$X> on a <SAFE/priced> at <terms, if set — or:> we're letting
> the lead help set terms. Know your number; don't over-anchor on a call before
> you have leverage.

## "We just invested in something adjacent." (possible conflict)
> Appreciate you flagging it — is it close enough to be a conflict from your
> side? <If yes:> totally understand, and if there's a partner/firm you'd
> recommend instead, I'd value the pointer. Turn a conflict into a referral.

## When you don't know the answer
> I don't have that in front of me — let me get you the exact number today.
> Then actually follow up same day. A reliable follow-up is itself a signal.

## Closing every objection
Bridge back to momentum: "Does that address it? If so, I'd love to set up the
next step — a deeper dive with the team next week?"
