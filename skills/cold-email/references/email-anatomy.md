# Cold email anatomy — line by line

A reply-worthy investor cold email does four jobs in under 125 words: prove you
researched them, say what you do, prove it's working, make one easy ask.

## Subject line

- 3–6 words, lowercase-ish, concrete, zero hype.
- Encode signal: stage + category, or a mutual context + a metric.
- A/B two angles and let the founder pick:
  - Relevance angle: `Seed — <category> for <customer>`
  - Traction angle: `<metric> in <timeframe> — raising seed`
  - Warm-ish angle: `<mutual person/community> + <one-liner>`
- Avoid: exclamation marks, "quick question", "opportunity", "revolutionary".

## Line 1 — the hook (why *them*)

The single most important sentence. It must be specific and true:
- "I saw <fund> led <company>'s seed — we're building the <X> for <Y>, and I
  think the same thesis applies."
- "Your post on <topic> is exactly the problem we're solving for <customer>."
- "You backed <company> early; we're going after the adjacent <wedge>."

If you can't write this line truthfully, you haven't done the research yet.

## Line 2 — what you do

One sentence. No jargon, no buzzwords. A smart outsider should get it.
- "We help <customer> <do job> without <pain>."

## Lines 3–4 — traction (the proof)

Lead with the hardest evidence you have. Numbers beat adjectives.
- Revenue/growth: "$X ARR, growing Y% MoM for N months."
- Usage: "N teams using it weekly, Z% retained at 8 weeks."
- Demand: "12 signed LOIs incl. <notable>, before launch."
- If pre-revenue: a credible design partner, waitlist, or a sharp insight from
  lived experience. Be honest about being early — investors fund early all the
  time; they don't fund inflated.

## Line 5 — the ask

One concrete, low-friction request:
- "Open to a 20-minute call next week? Tue/Thu afternoon work on my end."
- Offer the materials rather than attaching: "Happy to send the deck if useful."

## Signature

Name · role · company · ONE link (site or deck). No attachments on first touch
(attachments hurt deliverability and feel presumptuous).

## Full example (B2B SaaS seed)

> **Subject:** Seed — onboarding automation for fintechs
>
> Hi <Name>,
>
> I saw <Fund> led <PortfolioCo>'s seed — we're building the compliance-
> automation layer that companies like them end up needing, and your thesis on
> <topic> is exactly why I'm reaching out.
>
> We help fintechs cut customer onboarding from days to minutes without adding
> compliance headcount. Since January: $22K MRR growing ~30% MoM, 9 paying
> customers including <Notable>, 94% logo retention.
>
> We're raising a $2.5M seed. Open to a 20-minute call next week — Tue or Thu
> afternoon works well for me?
>
> Best,
> <Name> · CEO, <Company> · <link>

(~95 words.)

## Follow-up templates

**Follow-up 1 — ~4–5 business days later, add new info:**

> Hi <Name> — quick follow-up with fresh news: we just <new milestone: signed
> <customer> / crossed $X / shipped <thing>>. Still think there's a strong fit
> with <Fund>'s work in <space>. Worth 20 minutes?

**Follow-up 2 — ~7 days later, shorter + graceful out:**

> Hi <Name> — last note from me so I'm not crowding your inbox. If a seed-stage
> <category> company isn't a fit right now, totally understand — and if there's
> someone on your team or network better suited, a quick pointer would mean a
> lot. Thanks either way. <Name>

Then stop. Honor any "no" or "not now" immediately.

## Common mistakes that kill replies

- A wall of text / multiple paragraphs of backstory.
- No specific reason you emailed *this* investor.
- Hype adjectives instead of numbers.
- Multiple asks ("call, and also intros, and also feedback on the deck").
- A huge attachment on the first email.
- Obvious mail-merge tokens or a generic "Dear investor".
